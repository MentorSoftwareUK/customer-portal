Why our dashboard MRR is more accurate than HubSpot's native report


Written for: Operations & Marketing Managers
Date: 25 March 2026

The MRR figure on our admin dashboard will not match the number in HubSpot's built in Revenue Analytics report. Our number is more accurate. This note explains the difference and why it matters.


What is MRR?

Monthly Recurring Revenue is the total amount in pounds we can expect to receive every month from all currently paying customers. The key word is "currently". Revenue from customers who have left is not recurring revenue.


The problem with HubSpot's native MRR

HubSpot's report adds up the hs_mrr value from every closed won deal, across all time, regardless of whether the customer is still with us. It has no concept of a "live customer". A company that signed up in 2022 and cancelled in 2023 still contributes to HubSpot's MRR total today. That means HubSpot's number includes revenue we are not receiving and will never receive again.

As your customer base matures and natural churn accumulates, this gap gets worse. Every customer who leaves stays in HubSpot's total permanently. After a few years, HubSpot's MRR figure can be significantly inflated by dozens of companies that are no longer paying.

HubSpot also counts deals across all pipelines, which can include test deals, partner deals, or other non subscription revenue.


What our dashboard does differently

Our calculation applies two filters that HubSpot does not.

First, we only count companies whose sales status is currently set to "Paying Customer" in HubSpot. The moment a company churns and their status is updated, their MRR drops out of our number. This is the single biggest improvement over HubSpot's report. It means our figure reflects what we are actually being paid right now, not what we were being paid at some point in the past.

Second, we only count deals from the default sales pipeline. Test pipelines, partner pipelines, and anything else that is not a real subscription are excluded.

The result is a number that answers the question managers actually care about: how much recurring revenue are we receiving this month from customers who are still with us?


Why some customers have more than one deal

As customers grow and add more homes, their subscription value increases. This is typically recorded as a new deal with the updated hs_mrr value. The old deal should then be zeroed out so only the current amount is counted. When the data is maintained correctly, each paying customer has exactly one deal with a non zero hs_mrr, reflecting what they are actually paying today.


Keeping the data clean

There is one area where data quality matters. When a customer renews or upgrades, a new deal is created with the updated hs_mrr value. The old deal should then have its hs_mrr set to zero so it is not counted alongside the new one. Hope handles this as part of the renewal process.

If that zeroing step has not happened yet, both the old and new deal will temporarily be counted, which overstates that customer's MRR. This is a timing issue with the data entry, not a flaw in the calculation. Once Hope zeros the superseded deal, the number corrects itself on the next refresh.

To put this in perspective: HubSpot's native report has the same exposure to this issue and also has the much larger problem of counting every churned customer forever. Our approach is strictly more accurate.


The formula in plain English

Our MRR equals the sum of hs_mrr from all closed won deals in the default pipeline, but only for companies whose sales status is currently "Paying Customer", and only where the deal was won on or before the current month.

HubSpot's MRR equals the sum of hs_mrr from all closed won deals in all pipelines, regardless of whether the customer is still paying or left years ago.


A worked example

Imagine we have three companies.

Company A is a paying customer with one deal at £300 per month. Company B churned last month but still has a deal at £150 per month in HubSpot. Company C is a paying customer who originally signed up at £200 per month but later added more homes and now pays £250 per month. The new deal has the correct hs_mrr of £250 and the old deal has been zeroed out.

HubSpot's report shows £300 + £150 + £0 + £250 = £700 per month. That includes Company B, who is no longer paying us.

Our dashboard shows £300 + £250 = £550 per month. Company B is excluded because they are no longer a paying customer. Company C's old deal is zeroed so only the current £250 is counted.

£550 is the correct figure. £700 is not.


The MRR trend line

The 12 month trend chart on the Sales tab works the same way. For each month, we look at which companies were paying customers at that point and sum their deal MRR values. This gives a true picture of how recurring revenue has grown or shrunk over time, because churn is reflected as it happens rather than being silently ignored.


What to check if the numbers seem off

If our number seems too high, the most likely cause is a renewal where the old deal has not been zeroed yet. Ask Hope to check for paying companies with more than one deal carrying a non zero hs_mrr for the same product.

If a company has churned but their sales status has not been updated to "Past Customer", their MRR will still appear in our total. Updating the status in HubSpot fixes this immediately.

If a deal is missing its hs_mrr value entirely, it contributes £0 until the field is populated.

In each case the fix is a data update in HubSpot. The calculation logic is sound.


Summary

HubSpot's native MRR report permanently counts every customer who ever signed up, including those who left months or years ago. Our dashboard only counts customers who are paying right now, using only the relevant sales pipeline. That is why our number is lower than HubSpot's and why it is the more trustworthy figure for understanding actual recurring revenue.
